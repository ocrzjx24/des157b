Works Cited

Gibson, Lindsay et al. Historical thinking: Trends, critiques, and future directions.. Collective Memory, 2025.

Gibson and her colleagues here explore what people of the past expected to happen in their future (otherwise known as our present), why they might've thought in such ways, and what came true or not. They also discuss historical thinking (HT) in the form of how people of the present look back upon the past. Ultimately, there exists a lot of variance between both, and in particular, people now look upon the past with less reverence and esteem than vice versa; lofty expectations from the past, even if combined with warnings of slippery slopes to dystopia, were generally more optimistic. I think this dissonance in viewpoints can play into my project and be something to explore and surprise viewers with.

Kessler, Oliver, and Halvard Leira. “The Future Is Just Another Past.” Review of International Studies 50.3 (2024): 425-440.

Kessley and Leira investigate trends of the present that have existed since the past. As they put it, "no concept emerging in the 19th century has remained untouched." In particular, a lot of what was expected came true, and a lot of tasks we do now would have taken people of a past age far longer to do; speed is one of the most prevalent differentiators of eras. I think that this, being of course primarily impacted by technological improvements, could play into a larger narrative of how tech is advancing so fast, especially when motivated by money. I think this could help the viewer of my project raise thought-provoking questions for themselves.

Smolla, Rodney. Michigan Law Review Michigan Law Review the Life of the Mind and a Life of Meaning: Reflections on the Life of the Mind and a Life of Meaning: Reflections on Fahrenheit 451.. 2009.

Smolla analyzes and reflects upon Fahrenheit 451, a dystopian novel written in the 50s that offered a very cynical and honestly frightening view of the future. However, several predictions made in the book have come true in recent years. Smolla looks to talk about why, despite having Bradbury's warnings, society still allowed for these changes to occur (especially in regards to censorship and political propaganda). I think that presenting such a strange story to viewers, then having them make the connection between what was once seen as outlandish with what has been happening in reality, could be quite interesting.
